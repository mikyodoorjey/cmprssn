The Organizational Ceiling

You swapped the motor. You haven't redesigned the factory.

In the 1890s, textile mills replaced steam engines with electric motors and saw no productivity gains for thirty years. The gains came only when they redesigned the factory floor, the roles, and the assembly line itself. As George Sivulka wrote in a16z, "We've swapped the motor; we have not yet redesigned the factory."

Your organization is a complex system designed for humans at human speed. The workflows, roles, decision rights, and culture all assume a person is in the loop at every step. When you deploy autonomous agents into that system, they inherit its constraints: structural and cultural. The unspoken norms, the identity dynamics, the institutional logic that determines what the organization is willing to absorb.

"Productive individuals do not make productive firms." Individual gains do not compose at the institutional level, because the system was never restructured to absorb them.
after George Sivulka, "Institutional AI vs Individual AI," a16z (March 2026)

Most transformation programs get the order wrong. They treat the technology as the variable and the organization as fixed. The agents get more capable every quarter. The architecture stays where it was. Individual speed rises. Institutional output flatlines. The rules the organization runs on have not been rewritten. They have been annotated. And annotating an outdated system with new tools is what we call assimilation: absorbing the new without changing shape.

Question 01
How are organizations actually integrating agents?
Not the strategy deck. The reality. Where does the technology sit, and where has the organization actually changed around it? We map the specific configurations of human-agent integration across industries and company sizes.
Question 02
Why do pilots fail?
95% of pilots never scale. 70% of failure is organizational. But "organizational failure" is not one thing. Is it culture? Structure? Governance? Politics? We distinguish the specific failure modes and where they cluster.
Question 03
Where does the money actually go?
BCG says 70% of value comes from people and process change. We want to know what percentage of spend actually goes there. The gap between where money goes and where value comes from is the market opportunity.
Question 04
What is happening below the radar?
Personal subscriptions, unsanctioned agent workflows, tools expensed without IT awareness. The gap between official strategy and actual usage is a diagnostic signal for how ready the organization really is.
Question 05
Who wins and who loses?
Role identity threat, quiet sabotage, the contradiction between "go fast" and "slow down." We surface the specific political dynamics: which roles feel threatened, which are expanding, and who the internal champions are.
Question 06

Do they see the competitive risk as real? Are regulatory deadlines accelerating their timeline or paralyzing it? What does their organization look like in three years if nothing changes?
